Building Your Trauma-Informed Care Team

This space is for reimagining healthcare interactions, starting with how we listen, validate, and empower patients to advocate for the respectful care everyone is entitled to receive.

About This Guide: Created with input from patients who have navigated medical trauma, this guide offers practical strategies for finding compassionate providers and building a supportive healthcare team. We recognize that identity, culture, and history shape how trauma is experienced and how care should be delivered.

Everyone Is Entitled to Respectful, Compassionate Healthcare

If you've ever felt your concerns minimized, dismissed, or misunderstood in healthcare settings, this guide is for you. Many people have experienced healthcare interactions that left them feeling unheard or even harmed.

What you experienced matters. Your concerns are valid. Compassionate care should be standard, not exceptional.

Content Note

This guide discusses experiences of medical dismissal and trauma. While we've been thoughtful about language, some content may resonate with difficult experiences. You have control over how much detail you engage with; all expandable sections are optional.

Part 1: Understanding Your Experience

Your Responses Are Valid

When healthcare interactions leave you feeling dismissed or unsafe, your body and mind respond protectively. These responses—whether anxiety, avoidance, or heightened vigilance—are natural reactions to difficult experiences, not personal failings.

Recognizing Patterns of Dismissal

+

Medical dismissal can take many forms. You might recognize some of these patterns:

Common Experiences:

  • Minimization: Having symptoms attributed to stress or anxiety without proper investigation
  • Interruption: Not being allowed to fully explain your concerns
  • Assumptions: Providers making judgments based on appearance rather than listening
  • Rushed interactions: Feeling pressured to condense complex concerns into brief visits
  • Lack of consent: Procedures or examinations without clear explanation or permission

How These Experiences Affect You:

  • Physically: Tension, sleep disruption, appetite changes around medical visits
  • Emotionally: Dread, anger, sadness, or numbness related to healthcare
  • Behaviorally: Delaying care, over-preparing for appointments, bringing support people

Remember: These are protective responses, not overreactions. Your body is trying to keep you safe.

What Trauma-Informed Care Looks Like

+

Collaborative Partnership

  • Providers who see you as the expert on your own body
  • Shared decision-making about treatment options
  • Respect for your knowledge and lived experience
  • Recognition of cultural, spiritual, and personal values in care planning

Transparent Communication

  • Clear explanations in accessible language
  • Permission-seeking before examinations or procedures
  • Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty when it exists
  • Willingness to answer questions without defensiveness

Respect for Autonomy

  • Honoring your right to decline or delay decisions
  • Respecting requests for specific accommodations
  • Supporting your choices even when they differ from recommendations
  • Recognizing that you can change your mind

Part 2: Preparing Yourself

Create Your Care Preferences Statement

A care preferences statement helps providers understand how to work effectively with you. Think of it as a collaboration guide clarifying what supports your ability to engage fully in your care.

Building Your Statement

+

Elements to Consider Including:

Environmental Preferences:

  • Seating arrangements that feel comfortable
  • Having a support person present
  • Door positioning (open or closed)
  • Taking breaks when needed

Communication Preferences:

  • Written summaries of discussions
  • Time to process before responding
  • Questions welcomed and encouraged
  • Simple language over medical jargon

Examination Preferences:

  • Explanation before any physical contact
  • Permission-seeking for each step
  • Option to stop or pause at any time
  • Specific positioning accommodations
Introduction Script

"I've found that I participate most effectively in my healthcare when certain approaches are used. I've prepared a brief summary of what helps me feel comfortable and engaged. Could we review this together?"

Boundary Setting

"I appreciate you taking time to understand my needs. When I feel rushed or unclear about what's happening, it's harder for me to share important information about my symptoms. Can we approach this at a pace that works for both of us?"

Finding Trauma-Informed Providers

+

Search Strategies:

  • Professional directories: Filter by specialties like "trauma-informed care" or "culturally responsive care"
  • Community networks: Connect with support groups or advocacy organizations
  • Peer recommendations: Ask trusted friends or online communities
  • Healthcare systems: Look for institutions with stated commitments to patient-centered care

Initial Screening Questions:

Phone Screening

"I'm looking for a provider who takes a collaborative approach to healthcare. Can you tell me about Dr. [Name]'s philosophy on working with patients who have specific communication needs?"

Green Flags to Look For:

  • Office staff who are patient with questions
  • Flexibility in appointment scheduling and length
  • Willingness to accommodate support persons
  • Clear communication about practice policies
  • Positive responses to trauma-informed care inquiries

Part 4: Navigating Challenges

When Interactions Feel Difficult

Even with preparation, some healthcare interactions may feel challenging. Having strategies ready can help you maintain your autonomy while protecting your wellbeing.

In-the-Moment Strategies

+

Grounding Techniques:

  • Focus on your breathing (4 counts in, 6 counts out)
  • Press your feet firmly into the floor
  • Hold a small comfort object
  • Name 5 things you can see in the room

Communication Resets:

Pause Request

"I need a moment to collect my thoughts. Can we pause for just a minute?"

Clarification Request

"I want to make sure I understand correctly. Could you explain that again in simpler terms?"

Knowing When to Step Back:

It's okay to:

  • Request to continue the discussion at another appointment
  • Ask for time to consider treatment options
  • Bring in a support person for the next visit
  • Seek a second opinion if you feel unheard
  • Change providers if the relationship isn't working

After Difficult Appointments

+

Immediate Self-Care:

  • Move to a space where you feel safe
  • Connect with a supportive person
  • Engage in grounding activities
  • Remind yourself that the difficult experience is over

Processing the Experience:

  • Write down what happened while it's fresh
  • Note what felt difficult and why
  • Identify what you might do differently
  • Consider whether to address concerns with the provider
  • Decide if this provider meets your needs

Moving Forward:

Every challenging experience teaches you more about your needs and boundaries. Use these insights to refine your approach and strengthen your advocacy skills.

Part 5: Your Ongoing Journey

Recognizing Your Growth

Healing and building confidence in healthcare settings is a gradual process. Celebrate each step forward, no matter how small.

Starting Your Journey Growing

Signs of Progress

You might notice:

  • Feeling slightly less anxious before appointments
  • Speaking up once when you previously would have stayed silent
  • Asking one question you wouldn't have asked before
  • Recovering more quickly from difficult interactions
  • Feeling more confident in your right to respectful care

Remember: Progress isn't linear. Setbacks don't erase growth, they're part of the journey.

Ongoing Support Strategies

Building Your Support Network

+

Consider including:

  • Trusted friends or family: People who validate your experiences
  • Mental health support: Therapists familiar with medical trauma
  • Peer connections: Others navigating similar challenges
  • Online communities: Spaces for sharing and learning
  • Patient advocates: Professional support when needed

Self-Care Between Appointments

+

Regular practices that support your wellbeing:

  • Mindfulness: Brief daily practices to stay grounded
  • Movement: Gentle activities that feel good
  • Creative expression: Art, writing, or music for processing
  • Connection: Regular contact with supportive people
  • Rest: Prioritizing sleep and restoration

Resources & Tools

Quick Reference Scripts

Keep these phrases handy for common situations:

Setting Pace

"I process information best when we can go slowly. Could we take this one step at a time?"

Requesting Options

"What are all my options here? I'd like to understand the alternatives before making a decision."

Advocating for Needs

"This is important to my ability to participate in my care. How can we work together to accommodate this need?"

Crisis Resources

When you need immediate support:

  • 988: Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (24/7)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline: 1-800-656-4673
  • National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233

Additional Support

For ongoing resources and community:

  • Local patient advocacy organizations
  • Condition-specific support groups
  • Cultural or identity-based health organizations
  • Online forums for medical trauma survivors

Your Journey Continues

Building a trauma-informed care team is an ongoing process, unique to each person's experiences and needs. There's no timeline for healing, no "right" way to advocate for yourself, and no requirement to be perfect in your approach.

What matters is this: You have the right to healthcare that respects your autonomy, honors your experiences, and supports your wellbeing. Every step you take toward finding and creating that care is valuable.

Whether you're just beginning to recognize patterns of dismissal, actively building your care team, or supporting others on their journeys, your experiences and insights matter. Together, we're working toward a healthcare system that truly serves everyone with dignity and respect.
